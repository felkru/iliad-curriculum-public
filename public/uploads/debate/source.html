E.1 Debate Contributors Stephan Wäldchen (Independent) Learning outcomes The students: - Understand the difference between syntactic and semantic problems and why human judgment is necessary for a reward signal; - Understand the basic idea behind AI Safety via Debate as a Scalable Oversight Technique: If a task can be recursively decomposed in a debate, this technique allows us to extend a training signal on reasoning tasks into a regime where AI systems are smarter than humans; - Get an intuition about debate and cross-examination, and why they so drastically reduce the need for human oversight; - Understand the limitations inherent to the setup, and some of the open problems/counterarguments; - Are familiar with the UK AISI safety case, and are able to defend/critique it. Most important Take-Away: Debate is a form of scalable oversight: it seeks to extend a reward signal for honest solutions to difficult problems while reducing the amount of human judgment needed by an exponential factor. Prerequisites - Very important: How Turing machines work, and a basic understanding of the idea of computational complexity, including the most important classes, P, NP, PSPACE, the notion of NP-completeness, and polynomial-time reductions, Oracle Turing machines. - Medium Important: The difference between deterministic and non-Deterministic Turing machines, how mathematics is built on the axioms of set theory (used for most mathematics) or type theory (used mostly for proof checkers). Main content Read the description here for an intuitive idea, and for mathematical details and the exercises read the corresponding section in Debate_Proofs_and_Exercises.pdf (Google Drive 16A0hSaMEaBNUPYNvv6c2B5kp1lYfG550). Proof Checkers: When oversight works. A mathematical proof checker is a system that verifies whether a proposed proof is logically valid, step by step, relative to a fixed formal system. An example is Lean. But formal verification is limited to purely syntactic domains, and most problems are semantic, i.e., require world knowledge that needs to be checked by a semantic oracle, realistically either a trusted LLM or a human. Another limitation is that even in syntactic domains, proofs only work for solutions which can be verified with small (polynomially sized) proofs, so NP-type problems. The debate protocol aims to strongly reduce the size of the proof and the number of calls to a human judge. The Debate Setup. The original AISvD paper (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1805.00899)'s core idea is to align AI by having two systems debate each other in front of a human judge. The setup consists of a question, two provers Alice and Bob, and a human judge who evaluates a polynomially-sized list of arguments. It can be proved that this setup can solve all problems in PSPACE. Cross-Examination. Ordinary debate may let a dishonest debater get away with arguments that are only locally plausible. Cross-examination tries to fix this by allowing one debater, instead of giving a normal reply, to ask a question about something the other debater said earlier. The answer comes from a fresh copy of the earlier debater. The headline complexity result is that cross-examination increases the theoretical power of debate from PSPACE to NEXP. Criticisms: The Debate around 'Debate'. The most important is Obfuscated Arguments, raised by Beth Barnes: a malicious prover may decompose a false argument into subclaims where most are correct but a small number is false and it is computationally hard to find out which. Other criticisms: provers might get the human judge to run malicious code; no exploration guarantees in training; provers might collude; judges have biases. Prover-Estimator Debate. The paper (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.13609) is a direct attempt to solve the obfuscated arguments problem. Alice is the prover who decomposes a claim into subclaims, and Bob is the estimator who assigns probabilities. The paper introduces (ϵ,ρ)-stability: a recursive argument is stable if its correctness does not depend too delicately on tiny changes in subclaim probabilities. The AISI Safety Case. AISI's debate safety case (https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.03989) says: if debate can reliably make honesty the winning strategy, if training explores dishonesty enough to eliminate it, and if hidden flaws in obfuscated arguments can be handled, then debate could provide scalable oversight for advanced AI R&D agents. It decomposes the research agenda into assumptions that need evidence.
