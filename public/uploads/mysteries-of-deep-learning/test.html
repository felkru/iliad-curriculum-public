Learning outcomes

Goals:

  • Students can explain how the three barriers from Day B.1 (approximation, generalization, optimization) manifest specifically in the context of deep learning, and why classical theoretical frameworks (e.g. bias-variance tradeoff, universal approximation) actively counterpredicted deep learning's success
  • Students are aware of the key empirical mysteries of deep learning: data-dependent generalization despite overparameterization, effectiveness of SGD on non-convex landscapes, representational alignment across architectures, and in-context learning
  • Students have encountered at least one candidate explanation for each mystery and can articulate what it does and doesn't explain
  • Students understand the "program synthesis" hypothesis as one proposed framework connecting deep learning to Solomonoff induction, and can evaluate its strengths and limitations
  • Students can articulate why solving these mysteries matters for AI safety: understanding the basic mechanisms by which deep learning works is necessary for any systematic (generalizing OOD) alignment interventions or measurements to even be possible

Some notes on intentions:

  • As with Day B.1, the goal is awareness and broad context, not deep technical mastery. The subsequent days (SLT, training dynamics, influence functions) go deep on specific approaches; this day ensures students know what those approaches are trying to explain
  • The reading list is deliberately longer than can be comfortably covered in one day. The TA should select a subset based on audience interest and pacing. The "Deep Learning as Program Synthesis" post is the one reading that should not be cut, as it provides the overarching framework and connects back to Day B.1
  • This day is reading-and-discussion-heavy rather than exercise-heavy. That's appropriate given the survey nature of the content, but it means discussion quality matters a lot

Prerequisites

  • Day B.1 content: the three barriers (approximation, generalization, optimization), Solomonoff induction and the simplicity prior, no free lunch, bias-variance tradeoff
  • Basic understanding of deep learning, sufficient to read non-specialist ML papers
  • Knowledge of mechanistic interpretability (Day C.2) is very helpful motivation but not logically necessary

Content

Fast track

Read the lecture slides for the overall framing, then read "Deep Learning as Program Synthesis" (skipping the background section on Solomonoff induction, which was covered on Day B.1, and optionally deferring the "path forward" section). This gives a high level overview of various empirical mysteries. Then skim as many papers on the list as you have time/interest (possibly none).
