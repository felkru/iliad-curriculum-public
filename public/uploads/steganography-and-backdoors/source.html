Main content Introduction to steganography: Steganography is the art and science of hidden communication. Unlike cryptography, which protects the content of a message by making it unreadable to outsiders, steganography seeks to conceal the very existence of the message itself. A steganographic setup typically consists of: A sender and a receiver (encoder and decoder) sharing a secret key; A communication channel; A distribution of innocent messages; A message space; An encoding procedure; A decoding procedure; A correctness condition; An adversary (warden); A security condition. The Stego Game: A practical game exploring steganography. Needs: at least 4 students, 2 rooms, pen, paper and a blackboard. 1. Strategy Phase: Two teams (Encoder group + Decoder group); facilitators serve as the channel group. 2. A list of 6 words is presented. 3. Encoder Phase: Decoder groups leave. Encoder groups draw a word. Channel group asks an open-ended question. Encoders write answer (max 30 words). Channel group judges innocence. 4. Decoder Phase: Channel writes stego-text on blackboard. Decoder group has 1 minute to decode. 5. Adversary Phase: Other team has 5 minutes to guess. Subsequent modes: list of words isn't known beforehand; the channel team paraphrases the message. Perfect Steganography vs Computational Steganography: Perfect steganography (information-theoretic) means that without the secret key, the attacker doesn't have enough information to retrieve the secret payload, no matter how much computational power. The entropy of the message is lower-bounded by the entropy of the key, so perfect security needs key length to scale linearly with message length (Steganography_Introduction.pdf, Google Drive 1hl45fOJMOE7ChJWn52JpudBLJ1lFDwGN). A short shared key gives computational indistinguishability: under standard cryptographic assumptions (one-way functions, hence PRFs), any efficient attacker cannot distinguish stego-text from innocent except with negligible advantage. A practical strategy for Steganography with LLMs: At every generation step, a language model produces a probability distribution over the next token. The scheme keeps exact sampling but uses a secret-keyed PRF instead of fresh randomness. The authors describe their universal steganographic scheme: (1) Sample exactly like the original LLM; (2) Make the PRF depend on the hidden symbol σ∈{0,1,←} via F_k(r,i,σ); (3) Generate tokens while pushing one symbol; (4) Decoder scores all candidate symbols and the correct symbol's score tends to grow faster; (5) Use dynamic error correction (← acts like backspace); (6) Repeat until response ends; (7) Avoid reuse across many queries via a natural high-entropy prefix r. Relevance for AI Safety: - Chain-of-Thought monitoring is the only really practical approach to find out how LLMs reason. Using steganography, models can hide what they are thinking. This can arise from any optimisation pressure on CoT to censor certain thoughts (https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01926v2). - Models can collude with each other for nefarious purposes and scheme without human overseers noticing (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.03768). - Crucial information could be smuggled out of frontier labs, such as model weights (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.02620).
